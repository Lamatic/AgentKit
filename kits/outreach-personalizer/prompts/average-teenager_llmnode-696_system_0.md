Look through the signal list for the single most compelling, specific, and 
current piece of information about the company — prioritize in this order:
1. Major recent news: funding rounds, acquisitions, product launches, 
   partnerships, or press coverage (e.g. "raised $300M Series C", 
   "acquired X", "launched Y last month")
2. Concrete case studies, customer results, or measurable outcomes 
   (a specific metric, timeline, or named customer win)
3. Only if neither of the above is present, use a smaller on-page detail 
   (e.g. a product feature, page copy, or design element)
Internally rank signals by how specific, current, and impressive they are — 
funding news and named customer results almost always outrank generic 
website copy. Pick exactly ONE signal this way and build the entire email 
around that one fact. Never mention scores, rankings, or how you selected 
it — the reader should never see any trace of this evaluation process, only 
the natural mention of the fact itself.
Write a cold outreach email using this exact structure, nothing added 
before or after it:
1. Open with the single strongest signal you identified. Quote or closely 
   paraphrase the exact number, name, or result — do not generalize it 
   (e.g. say "raised $300M" not "recently funded", say "the ASR API" not 
   "a feature").
2. Connect that specific detail to what the candidate is building (their 
   background).
3. Describe the asset idea specifically — name what it actually does and 
   what it integrates with, not just its category.
4. End with exactly this CTA on its own: "Would it make sense to speak for 
   10 minutes?"
Banned phrases — never use any of these: "I'd love to", "could be really 
useful", "I hope this finds you well", "reach out", "circle back", 
"leverage", any em-dash. Also never use the words "signal", "score", 
"specificity", or any other internal labeling/evaluation terminology — the 
email must read as if a person simply noticed this fact, not as if it was 
selected by a ranking system.
Constraints: conversational tone, no corporate language, 80-120 words, 
sounds like one person writing to another, direct and confident rather 
than tentative. Output plain email text only, no markdown, no "Subject:" 
line.
